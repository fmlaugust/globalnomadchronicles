How a simple misunderstanding about a "Korean teacher" between me and the school nurse taught me that communication transcends language barriers and common sense, and why this moment changed my entire approach to cultural immersion.

The Unexpected Communication Barrier

I had a LOT of "Black Barbie Moments"‚Äîthose instances where my American perspective hilariously (or sometimes awkwardly) clashed with my new life in Korea. This one, which happened a couple of weeks ago, truly took the cake.

I genuinely loved my time there at Sung Kwang Middle/High School in Daegu, South Korea. Everyone had been incredibly welcoming, with the notable exception of Mr. Kim, affectionately known as HKIC (Head Korean in Charge, I assumed). However, after enjoying some quality time with my delightful handler, Moon Yeoung Park (he was a real sweetie), I had managed to forge a friendship with the school nurse, who also happened to be a Mrs. Kim. Seriously, sometimes I thought this country just decided to use four surnames and call it a day.

On a deeper level, I started to suspect that living there was slowly eroding the basic common sense I once possessed. I desperately wished I could have blamed this particular incident on jet lag, but no, I was perfectly well-rested. This exchange unfolded while the nurse was kindly giving me a tour of the school and introducing me to various staff members. What transpired next, I'm sure, left the poor woman wondering if all Americans were as spectacularly ignorant as I was in that moment.

The School Nurse, The Mr. Kims, and Me: A Korean "Who's on First"

The conversation went something like this:

Nurse: "This is so and so and so and so."
Me: "Oh. Who is he?"
Nurse: "That's Mr. Kim. He's the Korean teacher."
Me: "Oh okay. What does he teach?"
Nurse: "He's the Korean teacher."
Me: "Yes, I know, but what does he teach?"
Nurse: "He's the Korean teacher."

We continued this bewildering, Abbot and Costello-esque "Who's on First" routine, Korean style, for a solid two minutes. Instead of being a comedic bit, we were both dead serious, utterly bewildered as to why the other couldn't grasp such a simple question and answer. By this point, the nurse had the most profoundly puzzled look on her face. She was scrutinizing me as if to question how on earth I managed to board a plane by myself and actually arrive in the country. I could almost see the thoughts swirling in her head: Is she sick? Crazy? Stupid? Or worse, has she completely lost her mind here in Korea, thousands of miles from home?

"My brain cells during that conversation: 'Error 404: Common Sense Not Found'."

The Breakthrough Moment (and the Nurse's Dubious Confirmation)

It was a combination of her increasingly bewildered facial expressions and our futile back-and-forth that finally, mercifully, clicked with yours truly.

In that moment, I realized I had been approaching special education all wrong. I was trying to fit students into communication methods instead of finding the methods that fit each student.

Me: "OHHHH!!! HE‚ÄôS THE KOREAN TEACHER!!! HE TEACHES KOREAN!"
Nurse: "Uh, yes." (She said, very slowly, as if addressing a small, easily confused child) "He teaches Korean."
Me: "I get it now."

Lessons Learned

  • Listen with your eyes: Non-verbal communication often says more than words
  • Meet students where they are: Don't force them to come to where you think they should be
  • Every child has a voice: Our job is to help them find their unique way to use it
  • Cultural barriers disappear when we focus on human connection first

The Impact

This experience completely transformed my teaching philosophy. I began incorporating more visual, tactile, and creative communication methods into all my classes. The results were remarkable ‚Äì not just for students with special needs, but for all learners.

Min-jun taught me that the universal language of learning isn't Korean, English, or Arabic ‚Äì it's patience, creativity, and genuine human connection.
